Designing with Dark Mode in Mind

April 20, 2026 · 4 min read

Dark mode has gone from a niche preference to an expected feature. Whether it's saving battery on OLED screens or reducing eye strain in low-light environments, users increasingly expect the option. But designing for dark mode isn't just inverting colors — it's a thoughtful design process.

Don't Just Invert

The most common mistake is simply inverting the color palette. Pure white text on pure black backgrounds creates harsh contrast that's actually harder to read. Our eyes aren't designed for that level of luminance difference.

Instead of black (#000), use a very dark gray like #121212 or #1A1A2E. Instead of pure white, use off-white shades like #E0E0E0 or #F0F0F0. This reduces eye strain while maintaining readability.

Respect Color Saturation

Colors that look vibrant and friendly on a light background can become painfully bright on a dark background. Reduce saturation for your dark mode palette. A good rule of thumb is to desaturate accent colors by 20–30% and avoid using highly saturated colors for large areas.

This is especially important for the pink and purple gradients used on this very site. On dark mode, they need to glow but not overwhelm.

Depth Through Layering

In light mode, shadows create depth. In dark mode, use luminance instead. Elevate elements by making them lighter:

  • Surface 0 (background): #0A0A14
  • Surface 1 (cards): #141428
  • Surface 2 (elevated): #1E1E3A
This creates a clear visual hierarchy without relying on box shadows that look unnatural in dark mode.

Test, Test, Test

Always test your dark mode implementation across different devices and lighting conditions. A design that looks great on an OLED phone screen at midnight might look washed out on an IPS monitor in a dimly lit room.

Use browser DevTools to simulate different contrast ratios and ensure your text meets WCAG AA standards (4.5:1 for normal text, 3:1 for large text) in both modes.

Provide the Toggle

Let users choose. Some people prefer dark mode even in bright environments, and some prefer light mode at night. A simple toggle in the navigation (like the moon/sun icon on this site) with prefers-color-scheme detection gives everyone the best experience.

Dark mode isn't just a trend — it's an accessibility feature that shows your users you care about their comfort. Design it intentionally and they'll notice the difference.
