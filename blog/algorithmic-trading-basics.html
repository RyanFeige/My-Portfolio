Getting Started with Algorithmic Trading

May 1, 2026 · 6 min read

Algorithmic trading sounds like something reserved for Wall Street quant firms with billion-dollar budgets. The truth is, with modern tools and APIs, anyone with programming skills can start building and testing trading algorithms from their laptop.

What Is Algorithmic Trading?

At its core, algorithmic trading is simply using computer programs to execute trades based on predefined rules. These rules can be as simple as "buy when the 50-day moving average crosses above the 200-day moving average" or as complex as machine learning models analyzing sentiment from thousands of news articles.

The key advantage is removing emotion from trading decisions. Computers don't panic during a dip or get greedy during a rally — they follow the strategy exactly as programmed.

What You Need to Get Started

You don't need a finance degree. Here's the essential toolkit:

  • A programming language — Python is the most popular choice for algo trading
  • A brokerage with API access — Alpaca, Interactive Brokers, and TD Ameritrade all offer APIs
  • Market data — Yahoo Finance (via yfinance) is free for basic use
  • A backtesting engine — Backtrader, Zipline, or custom scripts

Start with Backtesting

Before you risk a single dollar, backtest your strategy against historical data. This is where the real learning happens. You'll discover how your strategy would have performed in different market conditions — bull runs, crashes, sideways markets.

A simple moving average crossover strategy in Python might look like this:

import pandas as pd\nimport yfinance as yf\n\ndata = yf.download('AAPL', start='2020-01-01')\ndata['SMA50'] = data['Close'].rolling(50).mean()\ndata['SMA200'] = data['Close'].rolling(200).mean()\ndata['Signal'] = 0\ndata.loc[data['SMA50'] > data['SMA200'], 'Signal'] = 1

Risk Management Is Everything

The best algorithm in the world will fail without proper risk management. Always consider position sizing, stop-losses, and maximum drawdown limits. Never risk more than you're willing to lose on any single trade or strategy.

Start Small

My advice: paper trade (simulate) for at least three months. Track every trade. Analyze what works and what doesn't. Only then consider deploying real capital — and start with an amount you'd be comfortable losing entirely.

Algorithmic trading is a journey, not a destination. The markets evolve, and your strategies need to evolve with them. Enjoy the process of learning and building.
